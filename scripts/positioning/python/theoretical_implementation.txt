\subsubsection{Cartesian-Frame Mechanisation Equations} \label{subsubsection:nav_eq}

For the Cookie sensor body frame (Figure \ref{fig:imu_axis}), the position and velocity vectors expressed in the local \ac{enu} navigation frame are defined as $\mathbf{p}^n = [p_E, p_N, p_U]^T$ and $\mathbf{v}^n = [v_E, v_N, v_U]^T$, respectively. The orientation of the sensor relative to the navigation frame is represented by the Euler angle vector $\bm{\epsilon}^n=[\phi,\theta,\psi]^T$, corresponding to roll, pitch, and yaw.

Following \cite{groves_gps} with local Cartesian frame approach. The Position Rate becomes: $\dot{\mathbf{p}}^n = \mathbf{v}^n$ (No $R_M$ or $R_N$ needed). The Transport Rate ($\bm{\omega}_{ne}^n$) becomes zero (because the frame doesn't tilt as you move). The Coriolis term ($2\bm{\omega}_{ei}^n \times \mathbf{v}^n$) is often neglected for low-cost sensors because it is much smaller than the sensor noise.

The evolution of the \ac{ins} state is described by a system of first-order differential equations that represent a sequential transformation from raw sensor data to a 3D navigation solution. To ensure a robust representation, all navigation equations utilise the coordinate transformation matrix (or \ac{dcm}) for attitude propagation, as this avoids the mathematical singularities associated with Euler angle rates at pitch angles of $\theta = \pm 90^\circ$.

The process begins with the attitude update, where the orientation of the sensor is propagated by calculating the time derivative of the \ac{dcm}, $\mathbf{R}_b^n$. This derivative is defined as the product of the current orientation and the angular velocity of the body relative to the inertial frame:
\begin{equation} \label{ec:dcm_rate}
\dot{\mathbf{R}}_b^n = \mathbf{R}_b^n \mathbf{\Omega}_{ib}^b
\end{equation}

The term $\mathbf{\Omega}_{ib}^b$ is the skew-symmetric matrix derived from the angular velocity vector $\bm{\omega}_{ib}^b = [\omega_x, \omega_y, \omega_z]^T$ measured by the gyroscopes. Following the standard cross-product mapping, this matrix is defined as:
\begin{equation} \label{ec:omega_skew}
\mathbf{\Omega}_{ib}^b = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\omega_z & \omega_y \\ \omega_z & 0 & -\omega_x \\ -\omega_y & \omega_x & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

The resulting transformation from the sensor (body) frame to the \ac{enu} navigation frame follows the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}_b^n$. For the Forward-Left-Up (FLU) body convention used in this work, the matrix is constructed using a $Z$-$Y$-$X$ (Yaw-Pitch-Roll) rotation sequence. In this convention, a nose-down pitch corresponds to a positive rotation about the lateral axis according to the Right-Hand Rule, resulting in the following definition for $\mathbf{R}_b^n$:
\begin{equation} \label{ec:Rbn}
\begin{aligned}\mathbf{R}_b^n &= \mathbf{R}_z(\psi) \mathbf{R}_y(\theta) \mathbf{R}_x(\phi) \\ 
\mathbf{R}_b^n &= \begin{bmatrix}\cos\psi&-\sin\psi&0 \\ \sin\psi&\cos\psi&0 \\ 0&0&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta&0&\sin\theta \\ 0&1&0 \\ -\sin\theta&0&\cos\theta\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0 \\ 0&\cos\phi&-\sin\phi \\ 0&\sin\phi&\cos\phi\end{bmatrix}\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

By establishing the updated $\mathbf{R}_b^n$ first, the system can then correctly transform the measured specific force into the navigation frame to resolve the subsequent velocity and position states.

This equation effectively integrates the gyroscopic rotation rates into a stable coordinate transformation, using the skew-symmetric matrix $\mathbf{\Omega}_{ib}^b$ derived from the body-frame angular velocity $\bm{\omega}_{ib}^b$. Once the updated orientation is established, it facilitates the transformation of the specific force. The raw acceleration measured in the body frame, $\mathbf{f}^b$, must be rotated into the \ac{enu} navigation frame to be meaningful relative to the Earth's surface. This transformed force is then used for the velocity update:
$$\dot{\mathbf{v}}^n = \mathbf{R}_b^n \mathbf{f}^b + \mathbf{g}^n$$

In this step, the transformed specific force is combined with the local gravity model, $\mathbf{g}^n = [0, 0, -9.81]^T$ m/s$^2$. By accounting for gravity in the vertical axis, the system distinguishes between the physical tilt of the train and its true linear acceleration. Finally, the position update is performed by deriving the rate of change of the local Cartesian position directly from the newly calculated navigation-frame velocity:
$$\dot{\mathbf{p}}^n = \mathbf{v}^n$$

This continuous chain of dependencies ensures that every change in the train's orientation correctly informs its trajectory in East-North-Up coordinates. To implement this on a digital processor, all states are integrated from time $t$ to $t + \Delta t$ using first-order Euler integration:$$x(t + \Delta t) = x(t) + \Delta t \cdot \dot{x}(t)$$


\subsection{Extended Kalman Filter Implementation}

Recursive filtering is particularly well-suited for resource-constrained extreme edge devices, such as the Silicon Labs EFR32 microcontroller utilised in the Cookies platform. The \ac{ekf} provides a closed-form solution for non-linear system dynamics. The \ac{ekf} assumes that both the state transition $f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{u})$, which describes the evolution of the system, and the observation model $h(\mathbf{x})$, which relates the measurements to the internal state, can be expressed as non-linear functions:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \bar{\mathbf{x}}&=f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{u}) + \mathbf{w}_x\\
        \mathbf{z}&=h(\mathbf{x})+\mathbf{w}_z
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{u}$ denotes the control input vector, $\mathbf{w}_x$ represent the process noise, and $\mathbf{w}_z$ the measurement noise.

These functions are linearised by calculating the Jacobian matrices around the current state estimate at time $t_k$, denoted as $\mathbf{x}_k$:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{F}&=\left.\frac{\partial f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{u})}{\partial\mathbf{x}} \right|_{\mathbf{x}_k,\mathbf{u}_k}\\
        \mathbf{H}&=\left.\frac{\partial h(\bar{\mathbf{x}})}{\partial\bar{\mathbf{x}}} \right|_{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_k}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This local first-order approximation enables the use of the standard \ac{kf} prediction and update equations. However, the \ac{ekf} requires the Jacobians to be re-evaluated at every iteration, ensuring the filter adapts to the varying dynamics of the non-linearity.

While some approaches define the input $\mathbf{u}$ as the raw \ac{imu} measurements, this thesis adopts an error-state \ac{ekf} formulation. The state vector $\mathbf{x}$ consists of 15 variables representing the navigation errors and sensor biases:

\begin{equation} \label{ec:vec_estado}
    \mathbf{x}=[\delta\mathbf{p},\delta\mathbf{v},\delta\bm{\epsilon},\mathbf{b}_{acc},\mathbf{b}_{gyr}]
\end{equation}
The position error $\delta \mathbf{p}$ and the velocity error $\delta \mathbf{v}$ are both expressed in the local tangent \ac{enu} frame to ensure consistency with the navigation reference. The attitude error $\delta\bm{\epsilon}$ represents the angular misalignment in Euler angles. To account for the noise of the \ac{mems} sensors, the vector includes the accelerometer bias $\mathbf{b}_{acc}$ and the gyroscope bias $\mathbf{b}_{gyr}$. These biases are estimated in the \ac{imu} body frame.

Therefore, the \ac{ekf} closed form is structured into two main phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Prediction:}
    \begin{equation} \label{ec:pred_EKF}
    \begin{aligned}
        \bm{\Phi}&=e^{\mathbf{F}\cdot T_s}\cong \mathbf{I}+\mathbf{F}\cdot T_s\\
        {\bar{\mathbf{x}}}&=\bm{\Phi}\mathbf{x}\\
        {\bar{\mathbf{P}}}&=\mathbf{\Phi P\Phi}^T+\mathbf{Q}
    \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Update:}
    \begin{equation} \label{ec:upd_EKF}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathbf{y}&=\mathbf{z}-\mathbf{H}\bar{\mathbf{x}}\\
        \mathbf{K}&=\bar{\mathbf{P}}\mathbf{H}^T(\mathbf{H}\bar{\mathbf{P}}\mathbf{H}^T + \mathbf{R})^{-1}\\
        \mathbf{x}&=\bar{\mathbf{x}}+\mathbf{Ky}\\
        \mathbf{P}&=(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{KH})\bar{\mathbf{P}}
    \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
where $\bm{\Phi}$ represents the discrete-time state transition matrix, $\mathbf{F}$ is the continuous-time system Jacobian matrix, $T_s$ is the sampling period, $\bar{\mathbf{P}}$ denotes the a priori predicted error covariance matrix, $\mathbf{P}$ is the a posteriori corrected error covariance matrix, $\mathbf{Q}$ is the process noise covariance matrix, $\mathbf{y}$ is the innovation or measurement residual, $\mathbf{H}$ is the observation Jacobian matrix, $\mathbf{K}$ is the Kalman Gain, $\mathbf{R}$ is the measurement noise covariance matrix, and $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity matrix.

The system evolution model $\mathbf{F}$ system dynamic matrix 15x15, \textcolor{magenta}{is based on \cite{groves_gps} and our Local-Cartesian-Frame Navigation Equations \ref{subsubsection:nav_eq}}, accounting for the coupling between orientation, velocity, and sensor biases:

\begin{equation} \label{ec:matriz_proceso}
 \mathbf{F}= \begin{bmatrix}
    \mathbf{0}&\mathbf{F}_{pv}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}\\
    \mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{F}_{v\epsilon}&\mathbf{R}_b^{n}&\mathbf{0}\\
    \mathbf{0}&\mathbf{F}_{\epsilon v}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{R}_b^{n}\\
    \mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&-\bm{\beta}_{acc}&\mathbf{0}\\
    \mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&\mathbf{0}&-\bm{\beta}_{gyr}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{0}$ denotes a 3x3 zero matrix.
    \item $\mathbf{F}_{pv}=\mathbf{I}_{3\times3}$ couples the position error to the velocity error.
    \item $\mathbf{F}_{v\epsilon} = [(\mathbf{R}_b^n \mathbf{f}^b) \times]$ is the skew-symmetric matrix of the specific force in the navigation frame, coupling attitude error to velocity.
    \item $\mathbf{F}_{\epsilon v} = \mathbf{0}_{3\times3}$ assumes a local tangent plane navigation model.
    \item $\mathbf{R}_b^{n}$ is the rotation matrix, used to transform coordinates from the \ac{imu} body frame to the \ac{enu} frame.
    \item $\bm{\beta}_{acc} = \text{diag}(1/\tau_{acc})$ and $\bm{\beta}_{gyr} = \text{diag}(1/\tau_{gyr})$ represent the time constants of the Gauss-Markov bias processes.
\end{itemize}
